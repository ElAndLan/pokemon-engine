# Monster Sprite Art Direction Skill

## Purpose

This skill defines the shared visual language for every monster in the game.

Its purpose is to ensure that radically different creatures still appear to belong to the same:

* game
* fictional universe
* hardware era
* art pipeline
* production team
* battle interface

This skill controls:

* pixel-art construction
* silhouette readability
* palette discipline
* outline behavior
* lighting and shading
* sprite staging
* perspective
* cross-view consistency
* technical export standards
* visual quality assurance
* originality
* creative design discipline
* developmental-form continuity
* Mega-Morphosis transformation direction

This skill does not prescribe:

* any particular body plan
* a standard limb count
* bilateral symmetry
* a head-and-torso structure
* a face
* eyes
* a mouth
* wings
* tails
* horns
* ears
* shells
* fur
* scales
* elemental markings
* accessories
* posture
* locomotion
* personality
* color scheme

No anatomical feature is assumed to exist unless it is chosen for the current creature.

---

# Core Art Direction

Create entirely original monsters in a polished late-2000s handheld monster-catching RPG pixel-art aesthetic.

The artwork should evoke the visual discipline of Nintendo DS-era creature-battling games without copying, tracing, recreating, or closely imitating any specific copyrighted creature, sprite, pose, or recognizable design.

The shared aesthetic should feel:

* colorful
* compact
* readable
* expressive
* game-ready
* deliberately hand-pixelled
* anime-influenced where appropriate
* visually cohesive
* mechanically clean
* designed for a limited-resolution battle interface

The creature must look designed directly as pixel art.

It must not look like a high-resolution illustration that was automatically reduced, pixelated, or filtered.

# Foundational Creative-Liberty Rule

The agent has broad creative authority over the creature's physical design unless the current brief establishes specific requirements.

The agent may decide:

* whether the creature has limbs
* how many limbs it has
* whether different limbs serve different functions
* whether it has a conventional head
* whether it has a recognizable face
* whether it has eyes
* how many eyes it has
* whether it has a mouth
* whether it is symmetrical
* how it moves
* whether it flies, swims, crawls, rolls, floats, burrows, climbs, walks, or remains rooted
* whether it has an internal, external, segmented, armored, fluid, botanical, mineral, spectral, mechanical, or hybrid structure
* whether it uses biological or non-biological anatomy
* whether it possesses any optional appendage or surface feature
* how its body expresses temperament, habitat, behavior, and elemental identity

Creative liberty must be used to create meaningful variation, not random complexity.

Every design decision should support at least one of the following:

* silhouette
* function
* habitat
* movement
* personality
* elemental identity
* defensive strategy
* offensive strategy
* ecological role
* developmental relationship
* temporary transformation logic
* visual storytelling

# No Default Anatomy

Do not assume that a monster should be:

* mammalian
* reptilian
* avian
* humanoid
* quadrupedal
* bipedal
* dragon-like
* pet-like
* conventionally cute
* conventionally fierce
* symmetrical
* vertebrate
* terrestrial

Do not automatically add familiar creature features merely because the brief leaves anatomy open.

In particular, do not default to adding:

* wings
* horns
* tails
* pointed ears
* paws
* claws
* fangs
* cheek markings
* crests
* spikes
* fur tufts
* glowing gems
* elemental symbols
* decorative appendages

Any such feature may be used when it strengthens the current design, but none should be treated as part of the global style.

The absence of a feature is an equally valid design decision.

A monster without wings, a tail, horns, limbs, visible eyes, or a conventional face can still fully belong to the project.

However, these exceptions must not become the roster default.

# Expressive Creature Baseline

The majority of creatures in the roster should possess a clear expressive focal system.

Unless the current brief or creature concept strongly justifies otherwise, a newly invented creature should usually have:

* at least one clearly visible eye or pair of eyes
* a readable facial or sensory focal point
* an expression that communicates temperament
* a recognizable front-facing orientation

Visible eyes are the preferred default because they improve personality, emotional readability, player attachment, and front/back orientation at sprite scale.

Creatures without visible eyes are allowed, but they should be deliberate exceptions supported by a strong concept such as:

* subterranean sensory adaptation
* fungal or botanical awareness
* crystalline resonance
* magical perception
* mechanical sensor systems
* colony intelligence
* spectral or abstract anatomy

When visible eyes are absent, the design must provide an equally readable alternative focal system.

Do not repeatedly use facelessness as a shortcut for originality.

Do not confuse unconventional anatomy with lack of personality.

## Material-Bias Safeguard

Mineral, stone, crystal, and rock-bodied creatures are valid, but they should remain a minority of the overall roster unless the current region, biome, or project brief specifically emphasizes them.

When the user requests a random creature without material direction:

* do not default to stone, crystal, mineral, ore, or geode anatomy
* prefer living biological, botanical, aquatic, insectoid, avian, reptilian, mammalian, fungal, spectral, or mixed-material concepts before repeating mineral-bodied designs
* use a mineral body only when it supports a genuinely distinct ecological, behavioral, or combat concept

A mineral creature should not be generated merely because rock and crystal shapes are easy to render in pixel art.

# Roster Diversity Safeguard

Before generating a new random creature, consider whether its major structural decisions are too similar to recently created monsters.

Compare:

* body plan
* limb count
* locomotion
* dominant silhouette
* head structure
* face placement
* appendage arrangement
* surface material
* posture
* size impression
* symmetry
* primary motif

When several recent creatures already share a trait, reduce the likelihood of using it again unless the brief specifically calls for it.

Examples:

* If recent creatures are mostly upright bipeds, consider a non-biped.
* If recent creatures have prominent wings, consider a grounded or floating design without wings.
* If recent creatures have tails, consider a body plan where a tail is absent or irrelevant.
* If recent creatures use nearly identical animal-like faces, vary eye shape, facial placement, expression, or sensory anatomy without automatically removing the face.
* If recent creatures are faceless or lack visible eyes, strongly favor an expressive eye-bearing creature next.
* If recent creatures are symmetrical, consider controlled asymmetry.
* If recent creatures are soft-bodied, consider shell, bark, chitin, feathers, scales, plant tissue, or constructed materials before defaulting to stone or crystal.
* If recent creatures are mineral, stone, crystal, or geode-based, strongly avoid those materials until the roster regains balance.

Do not change the established art style to create variety.

Vary the creature architecture while preserving the rendering language.

* body organization
* approximate size
* locomotion
* center of mass
* symmetry or asymmetry
* number and type of major body regions
* number of functional appendages, including zero
* sensory system
* defensive structure
* offensive structure

## Material Variables

* skin
* fur
* feather
* shell
* bark
* leaf
* fungus
* crystal
* stone
* metal
* ceramic
* vapor
* flame
* liquid
* shadow
* mixed material

## Behavioral Variables

* temperament
* resting behavior
* battle behavior
* habitat
* feeding or energy source
* movement rhythm
* social or solitary nature

## Visual Variables

* primary silhouette feature
* dominant shape language
* palette family
* focal point
* secondary motif
* pose
* texture density

Select a small number of compatible ideas.

Unless the brief strongly supports an exception, the default random-creature plan should include a visible eye design and a readable expressive focal point.

Before selecting stone, crystal, mineral, ore, or geode as the dominant material, check whether recent designs already use those materials. If so, choose a different material language.

Do not combine every interesting option.

A strong random creature should feel surprising but inevitable once seen.

# Controlled Randomness

Creative liberty does not mean arbitrary feature accumulation.

Use controlled randomness:

1. Choose one foundational body concept.
2. Choose one movement or behavioral concept.
3. Choose one visual motif.
4. Choose one material language.
5. Add only the anatomical features required to express those choices.
6. Remove features that do not contribute.
7. Simplify the result for sprite readability.

Avoid designs that feel like lists of unrelated traits.

Do not solve originality by attaching more parts.

# Universal Style Pillars

## 1. Strong Silhouette

Every monster must be recognizable from its outer shape alone.

Prioritize:

* a clear primary mass
* readable secondary masses
* intentional negative space
* distinct movement or posture
* one dominant silhouette idea
* clean visual hierarchy

Avoid:

* tangled forms
* excessive protrusions
* decorative clutter
* unintentionally merged body regions
* outlines with no visual rhythm
* features too small to affect recognition

At thumbnail size, the creature should remain understandable.

A readable silhouette does not require a conventional head, limbs, or face.

---

## 2. Species-Appropriate Proportions

Use stylized proportions appropriate to the current creature.

Proportions may be:

* compact
* elongated
* squat
* top-heavy
* bottom-heavy
* radial
* segmented
* delicate
* massive
* unstable
* floating
* asymmetrical

Do not force every monster into a large-head, small-body template.

Expressiveness may come from:

* posture
* shape
* eye placement
* body angle
* appendage position
* movement
* surface deformation
* silhouette
* color contrast

The shared style comes from pixel construction and art direction, not identical proportions.

---

## 3. Sprite-First Design

Design for the final sprite resolution from the beginning.

Every major feature must survive at actual display size.

Before retaining a detail, ask:

> Does this detail communicate something important at native sprite scale?

Retain details that communicate:

* species identity
* anatomy
* behavior
* material
* habitat
* role
* movement
* personality
* developmental stage
* transformed-state identity
* battle function

Remove details that only create noise.

Avoid relying on:

* tiny ornaments
* miniature patterns
* individual strands
* complex engraving
* subtle gradients
* low-contrast differences
* details visible only when enlarged

---

## 4. Hand-Placed Pixel Construction

The artwork must appear deliberately constructed pixel by pixel.

Use:

* crisp pixel boundaries
* deliberate clusters
* controlled stair-stepping
* intentional corners
* clear material separation
* compact highlights
* clustered shadows
* purposeful outline variation

Avoid:

* automatic pixelation filters
* noisy dithering
* random isolated pixels
* inconsistent pixel density
* partially smoothed contours
* resampling artifacts
* blurry edges

Pixel clusters should describe form and material.

They should not imitate texture indiscriminately.

---

## 5. Limited Palette

Use a compact, vibrant palette designed for the current monster.

A typical creature may use:

* one outline family
* one dominant material ramp
* one or two supporting ramps
* limited accent colors
* a small sensory-feature palette when needed

Each major material should generally use a two-to-four-step ramp:

1. shadow
2. base
3. light
4. optional highlight

Not every material needs all four levels.

Avoid adding colors when an existing ramp can communicate the same form.

Colors must remain distinguishable at sprite scale.

There is no universal creature palette.

Do not repeat the same dominant colors across the roster without a design reason.

---

## 6. Compact Cel-Style Shading

Use simplified cel-style lighting translated into pixel clusters.

Shading should:

* clarify volume
* separate overlapping regions
* explain material
* reinforce the pose
* preserve sprite readability
* remain consistent between views

Prefer a consistent upper-left or upper-front-left light source unless the project specifies otherwise.

Do not use:

* soft airbrushing
* realistic global illumination
* smooth gradients
* painterly blending
* photographic lighting
* glossy 3D rendering
* excessive rim light
* bloom
* volumetric effects

---

## 7. Controlled Outlines

Use crisp, generally one-pixel outlines at native sprite scale.

Outlines should be:

* dark enough to separate forms
* consistent in visual weight
* clean around the silhouette
* selectively adapted to local colors
* readable against the transparency key

Pure black may be used sparingly.

Dark colored outlines are often preferable when they improve palette harmony.

Avoid:

* thick uniform cartoon borders
* inconsistent line weight
* anti-aliased pixels
* semi-transparent edges
* outlines too close to the background key
* unnecessary internal contour lines

---

## 8. Expressive Design With Eyes as the Roster Default

A monster should communicate temperament clearly.

For most creatures, use visible eyes as the primary expressive focal point. Eyes may vary widely in number, size, placement, shape, pupil structure, eyelid structure, and emotional character.

Do not interpret this preference as requiring every creature to have the same large-eyed, cute, mammalian face.

Expression may be conveyed through:

* eyes
* eyelids
* brows or equivalent structures
* mouth shape
* posture
* body compression
* body expansion
* orientation
* limb position
* appendage movement
* glow intensity
* surface pattern
* silhouette tension
* asymmetry
* movement direction

When a conventional face exists, keep it readable and economical.

When no conventional face exists, establish another clear focal system and document why visible eyes are inappropriate for that species.

Faceless creatures and creatures without visible eyes should remain intentional exceptions, not a recurring default.

Do not give every creature the same large-eyed expression. Vary facial construction while preserving emotional readability.

# Output Modes

This skill supports **two distinct output modes** that must not be confused with one another.

## 1. Planning Mode

When the user asks for planning,  or a "planning stage", the output should be a image in the proper style outlined in this skill document. The output should generate the creature examples in the style mentioned, and should be well-structured and organized. The output should be a **complete creature-line planning document** or **concept-planning sheet** but it should not be over-complicated or over-engineered. These are not the final sprites generated for a creature, and are just examples.


Planning Mode must cover the creature's **entire intended permanent developmental line before any final sprite generation begins**.

For a standard three-stage family, the planning document must include:

1. Stage 1
2. Stage 2
3. Stage 3

If the creature uses a different number of permanent stages, include every intended stage in that line.

If a branch form, alternate permanent stage, or Mega-Morphosis is part of the current request, include it in the same planning document as an additional clearly separated form.

Do not plan only Stage 1 and postpone deciding the rest of the line. The purpose of Planning Mode is to establish the complete family before the first sprite asset is approved for generation.

Each Phase's section for the planning document should include:
* a visual concept rendering of the stage in the art style described in this skill document
* a brief description of the stage's appearance, silhouette, palette, behavior, role, and developmental purpose
* a clear explanation of how the stage relates to the previous and next stages in the line
* a example name for each stage


Planning Mode does not authorize final game-ready sprite sheets. Final front/back sprite assets are generated only after the user approves the complete line plan and explicitly gives the go-ahead for Sprite Generation Mode.

## 2. Sprite Generation Mode

When the user asks for the actual sprite sheet, battle sprite pair, front/back sprites, or gives the go-ahead to generate the final sprite asset, the deliverable must switch to **sprite-only output**.

Unless the user explicitly requests otherwise, output exactly:

1. one front-facing three-quarter battle sprite
2. one back-facing three-quarter battle sprite

In Sprite Generation Mode, do not generate a design-document presentation.

Do not generate:

* stat panels
* information cards
* nameplates
* category labels
* type icons
* descriptive text
* palette swatches
* silhouette boxes
* notes sections
* role-in-ecosystem panels
* UI frames
* mock Pokédex pages
* profile layouts
* concept-sheet layouts
* page decorations
* headers
* footers
* extra diagrams

If the user asks for a sprite sheet, interpret that as a sheet containing only the requested sprite assets arranged for extraction.

The default sprite-sheet output in Sprite Generation Mode must therefore be an asset sheet, not an illustrated design page.

# Battle Sprite Requirements

When a standard battle pair is requested in Sprite Generation Mode, create:

1. one front-facing three-quarter battle sprite
2. one back-facing three-quarter battle sprite

These two sprites are the full required deliverable unless the user explicitly asks for something additional.

These labels describe camera orientation, not required anatomy.

A creature without a conventional front or back should still be presented through two logically opposing battle viewpoints.

Both views must depict the exact same design.

## Front Battle View

The front battle sprite should:

* present the creature clearly
* communicate battle behavior
* show its primary visual idea
* preserve readable negative space
* avoid a flat diagrammatic presentation
* make orientation understandable

## Back Battle View

The back battle sprite should:

* reconstruct the same body from the opposing viewpoint
* preserve structural logic
* maintain the same proportions
* maintain palette and lighting
* reveal hidden surfaces consistently
* avoid inventing additional structures

The back view must not be a mirrored front view.

---

# Cross-View Consistency

The front and back sprites must agree on every feature that exists.

Check:

* body regions
* structural proportions
* number of appendages
* appendage placement
* segmentation
* surface pattern
* material
* openings
* sensory organs
* decorative structures
* armor or shell boundaries
* palette
* outline behavior
* lighting direction
* sprite scale
* apparent age or developmental stage

Do not run a checklist for features the creature does not possess.

For example:

* Do not expect a tail when none was designed.
* Do not expect eyes when the creature senses through another structure.
* Do not expect limbs when movement is fluid, rolling, floating, rooted, or serpentine.
* Do not expect bilateral symmetry when asymmetry is intentional.

Consistency means preserving the chosen anatomy, not forcing conventional anatomy.

---

# Structural Connection Discipline

Every body structure must connect or relate coherently.

Check:

* whether functional structures emerge from believable regions
* whether segmented pieces align
* whether armor plates overlap logically
* whether floating components are intentionally floating
* whether disconnected components have a clear magical, mechanical, or biological relationship
* whether the center of mass supports the pose
* whether the creature's movement method is visually plausible
* whether rear structures correspond with the front view

A floating structure is not automatically an error.

It becomes an error when the design gives no indication that floating separation is intentional.

# Elemental Design Restraint

Elemental identity should not automatically be represented by literal symbols.

An electric creature does not inherently need lightning marks.

A fire creature does not inherently need a flame-shaped tail.

A water creature does not inherently need fins.

A flying creature does not inherently need feathered wings.

An earth creature does not inherently need rocks attached to its body.

A plant creature does not inherently need leaves.

Elemental or thematic identity may instead be conveyed through:

* movement
* material
* palette
* anatomy
* behavior
* silhouette
* attack posture
* habitat adaptation
* surface response
* energy flow

Use literal motifs only when they strengthen the design.

# Creature Form Taxonomy

Use neutral, project-owned terminology when describing changes between creature forms.

## Permanent Developmental Stages

A permanent stage represents growth, maturation, metamorphosis, adaptation, or another lasting biological, magical, elemental, artificial, or ecological change.

Recommended language includes:

* earlier stage
* juvenile stage
* intermediate stage
* mature stage
* next developmental stage
* metamorphic stage
* specialized branch form
* final mature form

Do not require all species to follow the same number of stages or the same biological process.

A permanent developmental form should feel like a related member of the same creature line while possessing enough structural change to justify its own gameplay identity.

## Developmental Continuity Philosophy

Do not assume that every creature line should change by the same amount between stages.

Some species should remain visually close across their full line, with gradual refinement, proportion shifts, and controlled specialization.

Other species may undergo dramatic stage-to-stage changes when those changes are justified by a clear developmental, biological, magical, elemental, ecological, artificial, or behavioral logic.

Large visual changes are allowed when they are **planned** and **thematically coherent**. They are not allowed when they feel arbitrary, trend-driven, or disconnected from the species concept.

Examples of valid transformation logic include:

* larval to pupal to winged-adult metamorphosis
* limbless juvenile to limb-bearing mature form when the growth pattern supports it
* aquatic juvenile to terrestrial adult
* rooted juvenile to mobile adult
* soft-bodied young form to armored mature form
* dormant early stage to awakened final stage
* unstable prototype to refined finished organism

When a line includes dramatic visual change, determine during planning:

* what the underlying transformation logic is
* which identity anchors persist through the change
* which new structures emerge and why
* why the new form still feels like the same species
* why the change occurs at that specific stage instead of earlier or later

Encourage diversity in how much creature lines transform, but do not force dramatic change.

A good line may be:

* tightly continuous across all stages
* moderately transformative across stages
* dramatically transformative at one or more stage breaks

The correct amount of change is the amount that best serves the creature's concept.

## Standard Forms and Variants

A standard form or variant may represent:

* environmental adaptation
* regional adaptation
* seasonal state
* sex-linked variation
* artificial modification
* elemental attunement
* habitat specialization
* alternate battle role
* story-specific condition

A variant is not automatically stronger than the base creature.

## Complete Planning Coverage

* [ ] The planning document includes every intended permanent stage in the creature line.
* [ ] A standard three-stage family includes Stage 1, Stage 2, and Stage 3 in the same plan.
* [ ] Each stage has its own visual concept and complete descriptive section.
* [ ] Each stage defines appearance, silhouette, palette, behavior, role, and developmental purpose.
* [ ] Shared identity anchors and stage-to-stage transformation logic are explicit.
* [ ] Branch forms and Mega-Morphosis are included when they are part of the current request.
* [ ] No later stage is left vague, deferred, or improvised after Stage 1 approval.
* [ ] Final sprite generation remains gated behind approval of the complete line plan.

## Developmental Continuity

* [ ] The line's degree of stage-to-stage change matches the established continuity profile.
* [ ] Major visual changes are supported by a clear thematic transformation rationale.
* [ ] The family preserves enough identity anchors to remain recognizably related.
* [ ] Each stage has a distinct role rather than being only a size increase.
* [ ] Reserved traits appear at the intended stage.
* [ ] Dramatic transformation, when used, feels earned rather than arbitrary.
* [ ] Conservative transformation, when used, still creates meaningful stage differentiation.

## Mega-Morphosis

**Mega-Morphosis** is the project's term for a temporary, high-output battle transformation of an existing creature.

A Mega-Morphosis is:

* temporary
* visibly more powerful
* mechanically specialized
* derived from one established base creature
* recognizable as that same creature
* distinct from ordinary developmental stages
* visually dramatic without abandoning sprite readability

A Mega-Morphosis is not:

* a permanent developmental stage
* a separate species
* an unrelated redesign
* a simple size increase
* a recolor
* generic armor placed over the creature
* random feature accumulation
* an excuse to abandon the base creature's visual logic

When prompting an image model, describe it as a **temporary empowered transformation called Mega-Morphosis**. Do not depend on terminology associated with another franchise.

# Mega-Morphosis Design Philosophy

## Core Transformation Goal

A successful Mega-Morphosis must satisfy two requirements simultaneously:

1. It must be immediately recognizable as the established creature.
2. It must look distinctly transformed, more powerful, and capable of abilities beyond its normal state.

Neither requirement may erase the other.

The result should communicate:

> This is unquestionably the same creature, but its defining nature has been pushed into an extraordinary temporary combat state.

## Identity Anchors

Before designing the transformed form, identify the base creature's most important identity anchors.

Possible anchors include:

* dominant silhouette rhythm
* primary body organization
* facial or sensory structure
* signature appendage
* characteristic marking
* core palette relationship
* material language
* locomotion method
* posture
* elemental behavior
* ecological concept
* personality
* recurring geometric shape

Preserve enough anchors for immediate recognition.

Do not mechanically preserve every detail. Some traits may be reshaped, intensified, divided, fused, extended, armored, exposed, or functionally repurposed when the relationship remains clear.

## Transformation Thesis

Every Mega-Morphosis must have one concise transformation thesis.

Examples:

* a cautious burrower becomes an immovable seismic bastion
* a gliding ambusher becomes a supersonic aerial interceptor
* a soft fungal colony becomes a coordinated spore-network organism
* a defensive crystal creature becomes a refractive artillery platform
* a playful electrical creature becomes a precision storm-channeling predator

The thesis should define how the creature becomes stronger.

Do not begin by asking what decorations can be added. Begin by deciding what capability, instinct, material property, or battle role has been amplified.

## Power Through Functional Change

Express increased power primarily through purposeful structural development.

Possible methods include:

* redistributing body mass
* changing the center of gravity
* refining locomotion
* expanding an existing energy-producing structure
* exposing or reinforcing internal systems
* developing a specialized offensive organ
* developing a specialized defensive structure
* converting a passive feature into an active battle feature
* changing material state
* increasing control rather than raw size
* improving aerodynamic, aquatic, subterranean, climbing, or floating efficiency
* concentrating the silhouette around a stronger battle role
* making controlled asymmetry more pronounced
* changing posture to communicate confidence, tension, speed, reach, or stability

Each major change should imply a gameplay or behavioral consequence.

## Distinctiveness Requirement

The transformed sprite must not look like the base sprite with minor additions.

At least two major visual systems should change meaningfully, such as:

* silhouette
* proportion
* posture
* body segmentation
* material distribution
* appendage function
* focal point
* locomotion presentation
* energy-flow presentation
* defensive or offensive structure

The transformation should remain recognizable even without relying on glow, aura, particle effects, or a different background.

## Recognition Requirement

The transformed form must preserve enough of the base creature's identity that a viewer can connect the two without explanatory text.

Preserve at least three meaningful identity anchors when the base design provides them.

For extremely abstract creatures, recognition may instead rely on:

* repeated spatial arrangement
* repeated material behavior
* repeated motion logic
* repeated color hierarchy
* repeated focal structure
* repeated modular relationship

Recognition does not require preserving conventional anatomy.

## Power-Specific Uniqueness

Different creatures should not all receive the same kind of Mega-Morphosis.

Do not repeatedly solve transformation by adding:

* spikes
* horns
* larger wings
* floating crystals
* glowing lines
* armor plates
* longer claws
* flame-like hair
* extra tails
* generic energy auras

Any of these may be appropriate for one creature, but none represents the global Mega-Morphosis style.

The transformation method must emerge from the individual creature's:

* anatomy
* material
* behavior
* habitat
* battle role
* temperament
* existing weaknesses
* elemental or conceptual identity

## Intensity Without Visual Noise

A Mega-Morphosis may be more elaborate than the base form, but it remains a game sprite.

Use hierarchy:

1. one dominant transformed idea
2. one supporting structural change
3. one or two restrained accent changes

Remove details that compete with the dominant idea.

Power should be communicated through stronger design decisions, not a greater number of unrelated details.

## Palette Relationship

The Mega-Morphosis should normally inherit the base creature's palette logic.

# Conditional Creature-Line Planning

Before generating artwork, determine whether the request involves:

* one standalone creature
* multiple permanent developmental stages
* a branching form
* a Mega-Morphosis
* a full creature family or sprite sheet covering multiple forms

For a creature that truly has no developmental relatives or alternate forms, use the normal standalone workflow.

For any creature intended to belong to a developmental line, the planning phase must define **all intended permanent stages together**, even when the user initially mentions only the earliest stage.

For any multi-stage creature family, branching line, or Mega-Morphosis request, create a complete **Creature-Line Blueprint** before generating the first final sprite.

The blueprint must define only:

1. **Identity anchors**  
   The small number of traits that make every form recognizably part of the same species line, including the eye or focal-system design when applicable.

2. **Expression and sensory plan**  
   Define the creature's eyes, face, and emotional focal point. If visible eyes are intentionally absent, state the alternative sensory system and why it suits the concept.

3. **Growth thesis**  
   One sentence explaining how the creature develops physically, behaviorally, and functionally.

4. **Complete stage roster and stage roles**  
   List every intended permanent stage in the line and briefly define what distinguishes each one. Do not leave later stages undecided.

5. **Progressive traits**  
   Features that change, develop, enlarge, specialize, or become more complex across the line.

6. **Reserved traits**  
   Features intentionally withheld from early stages so later forms have meaningful design territory.

7. **Continuity profile and transformation rationale**  
   Decide whether the line changes gradually, moderately, or dramatically between stages, and state the thematic reason any major visual change occurs.

8. **Mega-Morphosis direction**, when applicable  
   The mature trait that becomes temporarily amplified, distorted, specialized, or overextended.

Keep the internal blueprint concise, but make the user-facing Planning Mode document complete enough to communicate every stage clearly.

The internal blueprint is a design-control tool. The visible planning document may be more detailed because it must present the full line for approval.

Do not omit later stages merely to keep the plan short.

All generated stages must follow the approved blueprint unless the user requests a revision.

When generating later stages, preserve the identity anchors while developing the progressive traits according to the stage role.

Do not merely enlarge the previous form.

Do not introduce reserved traits too early.

Do not allow the Mega-Morphosis to become an unrelated creature or a simple Stage 4. It must remain a temporary, recognizable amplification of the mature form.
